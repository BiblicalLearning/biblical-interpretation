Part Two

When Was This Written?

Understanding the historical timeframe transforms how we read Scripture.

Question 1 of 6

When was this written?

Why It Matters

A letter written before AD 70 carries different implications than one written after. Prophetic language, urgency of tone, and specific warnings all connect to the historical moment.

What to Investigate

  • The approximate date of composition
  • What was happening in the Roman Empire at that time
  • The state of the early church during this period
  • Whether Jerusalem and the Temple still stood

Example

When Paul writes to the Thessalonians about "the day of the Lord," knowing this was written around AD 50-51 ‚Äî nearly two decades before Jerusalem's destruction ‚Äî shapes our understanding of what "soon" and "at hand" meant to the original readers.

Historical Context Unlocks Meaning

Understanding that most New Testament books were written before AD 70 helps explain the urgency we see in passages about coming judgment, the "last days," and promises that things would happen "soon."

Part Three

Who Was the Original Audience?

Scripture was written for us but not to us.

Question 2 of 6

Who was the original audience?

A Crucial Distinction

Every letter had specific first-century recipients. Commands, warnings, and promises directed to a specific audience in a specific situation may apply differently to us today. Recognizing the original recipients prevents us from misappropriating texts.

What to Investigate

  • Were they Jewish believers, Gentile converts, or a mixed congregation?
  • What struggles or false teachings were they facing?
  • What was their relationship with the author?
  • What cultural context shaped their understanding?

Example

The letter to the Hebrews addresses Jewish Christians tempted to return to Temple worship. The urgent warnings about apostasy speak directly to their situation ‚Äî abandoning Christ to return to sacrifices that could never take away sin.

When we recognize who the original audience was, we can better understand why certain things were said and how those truths apply to our different context.

Application

Ask yourself: "If I were a first-century believer receiving this letter, what would I understand this to mean?" This prevents us from forcing modern assumptions onto ancient texts.

Part Four

What Is the Central Topic?

Every chapter and passage exists within a larger argument or narrative.

Question 3 of 6

What is the central topic?

Why It Matters

Pulling verses out of context leads to distorted theology. Understanding the flow of thought reveals the author's intended meaning. Identifying the main subject prevents proof-texting and misapplication.

What to Investigate

  • What question or problem is being addressed?
  • How does this section connect to what comes before and after?
  • What is the author's main point or argument?
  • Are there key words or phrases that repeat throughout?

Practical Approach

Before focusing on individual verses, read the entire chapter ‚Äî or better, the entire letter ‚Äî in one sitting. Summarize the main argument in your own words before examining details.

Example

Romans 8:28 ("all things work together for good") is often quoted in isolation. But in context, Paul is discussing the Spirit's work in believers, the certainty of future glory, and God's sovereign purpose in salvation. The "good" isn't general comfort ‚Äî it's conformity to Christ's image (v. 29).

Context is king. A text without a context is a pretext for whatever we want it to say.

Part Five

Who Is Speaking?

Scripture contains multiple voices.

Question 4 of 6

Who is speaking?

Why It Matters

Scripture contains multiple voices: authors, Jesus, Old Testament quotations, and sometimes opponents' views being refuted. The authority and application of a statement depends on who is speaking.

What to Investigate

  • Is this the author's own teaching?
  • Is Jesus being quoted directly?
  • Is the author quoting the Old Testament?
  • Is the author presenting a false view he's about to refute?
  • Is this descriptive (what happened) or prescriptive (what should happen)?

Example

In 1 Corinthians, Paul sometimes quotes slogans the Corinthians were using ‚Äî "All things are lawful for me" (6:12) ‚Äî before correcting their misapplication. Missing this leads to reading their errors as Paul's endorsements.

Descriptive vs. Prescriptive

Not everything recorded in Scripture is endorsed by Scripture. The Bible records lies, mistakes, and sins ‚Äî describing what happened without prescribing that we do the same. Just because Samson did something doesn't mean we should.

Part Six

Old Testament Fulfillment

The New Testament is saturated with Old Testament connections.

Question 5 of 6

Is this referencing Old Testament fulfillment?

Why It Matters

The New Testament is saturated with Old Testament quotations, allusions, and typological fulfillment. Recognizing these connections unlocks meaning.

New Testament authors interpreted Christ and his work through the lens of Old Testament prophecy, typology, and covenant promises. Missing these connections means missing their argument.

What to Investigate

  • Are there direct quotations from the Old Testament?
  • What was the original context of that Old Testament passage?
  • How is the New Testament author applying or interpreting it?
  • Is this presented as fulfilled in Christ's first coming, the early church, or the events of AD 70?

Key Principle

When a New Testament author says something "was fulfilled," take that claim seriously. The apostles, writing under inspiration, understood how prophecy connected to their present reality.

Example

Matthew frequently uses the phrase "that it might be fulfilled" to show Jesus as the culmination of Israel's story ‚Äî not merely predicting future events, but completing what God had promised.

Part Seven

Future Fulfillment

Determining what remains future requires careful study.

Question 6 of 6

Is this referencing future fulfillment?

Why It Matters

Some passages speak of events still future to the original audience. Careful study determines whether those events remain future to us or have since been fulfilled.

Misidentifying fulfilled prophecy as still-future creates confusion and misapplication. Conversely, recognizing what has been accomplished builds confidence in God's faithfulness.

What to Investigate

  • What time indicators does the text provide? ("soon," "at hand," "this generation," "in a little while")
  • Who is the "you" being addressed?
  • Have the described events occurred in history?
  • Does the New Testament elsewhere indicate this has been fulfilled?

Key Principle

Pay close attention to audience relevance. When Jesus tells his disciples "you will see" or "this generation will not pass away," he's speaking to them about their lifetime, not to readers two thousand years later.

Example

In Revelation, Jesus tells John the events "must soon take place" and "the time is near" (Rev. 1:1, 3). These time indicators, addressed to first-century churches facing Roman persecution, point to fulfillment within their horizon ‚Äî not ours.

Putting It Together: A Study Method

When approaching any passage, work through these steps:

  1. Begin with prayer ‚Äî Acknowledge your dependence on the Holy Spirit.
  2. Establish the timeframe ‚Äî When was this written? What was happening?
  3. Identify the audience ‚Äî Who first received these words?
  4. Find the main point ‚Äî What's the central argument?
  5. Determine the speaker ‚Äî Whose voice is this?
  6. Trace backward ‚Äî What Old Testament foundation is being built on?
  7. Look forward carefully ‚Äî If future, future to whom?
  8. End with reflection ‚Äî What is the Spirit teaching you?
